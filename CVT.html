Baja CVT Project

Project Overview

This is a project I recently did at Baja SAE. In the 2024 season, the stock CVT faced a lot of issues, mainly with traction/torque (struggles in hill climb) and overheating. To address this issue, I dove into the research and analysis of exactly what parts of the CVT were causing the traction issues. At last after weeks of reading paper after paper and creating traction curve after traction curve, the team looked at the research and determined the culprits were both the primary and secondary spring, as well as the flyweights. So, the flyweights and springs got swapped out and replaced with ones with the appropriate properties.

Calculations and Analysis


Primary Clutch:
Tuning the CVT to engage at an RPM that matches the engine's torque peak would allow for efficient power transfer and vehicle responsiveness. According to the following engine torque curve for the CX9, this was observed to be 2800 RPM.

Using this information, the RPM at which the clutch engages was measured with a tachometer at approximately 3000 RPM. This provided insight into the initial dynamics of power transfer between the engine and the drivetrain, and it was observed that the engagement RPM was slightly higher than the observed recommended average of 2800 RPM. This showed that there were issues with the CVT’s torque transfer and power and therefore demonstrated why hill-climbing was the 2024 vehicle’s primary weakness.


Lighter flyweights would delay the CVT’s shift into a higher gear, allowing for increased engagement RPMs. This would result in better acceleration and improved top-end speed, making it a preferred configuration for flat terrain and high-speed applications. Conversely, heavier flyweights would promote earlier engagement enhancing low-end torque for hill climbs and rugged terrain, addressing the late clutch engagement at 3650 RPM that was observed in the baseline data collection.
